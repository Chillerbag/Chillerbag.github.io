When you move house, it is necessary to imagine the act to enable you the capability for radical change. Otherwise the grandiosity of the expense and inconvenience would render it totally futile. Yet, you will find yourself arranging the draws in your kitchen with the exact same fuzzy delineation between the various utensils used for moving things around in crockery. The top draw is where your forks are. 
<br></br>
The top draw is mostly objective, not a matter of reasoning or conditioning. The forks go there because you need forks often, and the human spine is subject to long term consequence because you chose to be avant garde, or rather masochistic in where you placed your most frequently accessed items. The rest of the draws aren’t so clear though. I refuse to believe by the time you are unpacking boxes you are undertaking the rigorous and relatively meaningless dialectical exercise of ranking your countless utensils you didn’t know you had, including your 3 spatulas, by their order of frequency utilised, and converting that to their height relative to your standing position. My point is, you aren’t acting rationally to order them nine times out of ten, yet they are ordered in mostly the same way. I still instinctively know which draw to pull on, so I am superimposing the position of an entirely different place spatially onto this one. There is no overlap in shape or size, yet this leyline is carried with me, violently torn and reinstated. Instead of traversing this metaphysical enquiry, I morph it to my convention.
<br></br>
It’s pretty obnoxious to distil the idea of change to something so innocuous, because that is not what people are thinking. They’re thinking of their interpersonal relationships, breaking of bad habits, maybe a career change, maybe finally learning that software or that instrument. In this way I think everyone has some spirituality about their home as kind of a hyper-personalised religion. The space is a deity that defines your capability. You gradually become a non-believer, seeking out a new deity and worshippers around you to validate that belief. And through religion you are purified. It is only positive change you are seeking, after all. There’s objective reasoning like noise and violence, and through moving the escaping of such situations, but we keep score of these inflictions of material reality to re-purpose them into art, or just stories. The negative is something to flee and then embrace later on, which I think reduces the ability to fully utilise the feeling. Perhaps this is my capitalist brain converting something so ephemeral to some vegetable that can become easily spoiled, but it is that tenuousness of the human brain that cannot hold onto the emotions for so long that reduces the poignancy of the re-conveying of them. This is a all-around good feature though, because if we held onto our traumas then there would be no point in moving. The permanent haunt; you become irreconcilable and still find the same excuse to not convert the emotions and their excess energy into material reality. And you’d be a really boring person to be around. The manic euphoria of harmony with your own masochism dilutes it too, so perhaps the feelings we were running from are lost forever. Never elucidated in the archive of human experience in its raw energy, just distant echoes which sound similar. It’s why we love sob movies like Manchester by The Sea, which are characterised by nothing more than their agony. It refracts the radiation of our own emotions which are reaching their half-life and brings us in tune with our own suffering in a cosmic resonance with our self-gratifying entropic tendencies. It is the same reason we hold onto the objects an ex left us with when we move. There’s a part of us that wants to feel that agony forever, to keep yet another layer of reality we have formed above the material plane, which is that of memory. 
<br></br>
Some time last year, months after a pretty significant breakup, I returned to a town I had since avoided to return some items my ex left behind. I wasn’t thinking about it on the journey, but within my memory the town existed exactly as it did when we would slog through its main street on at least a weekly basis. The pub on the corner was being painted white, and I found it deeply alarming that I was in the town upon that observation. I refused to let the third layer of reality, above things like names, roads, suburbs, postcodes, which describe a place in the abstract like a database, be subsumed and replaced by this demon of material reality. I couldn’t let the simple tape of the human brain overwrite it even if it ultimately made the breakup less painful. After that, there’s been a number of restaurants, recipes, shops, etc I have tended to avoid for fear of summoning that demon which consumes dimensions. The agony is our maintenance of a different universe that we are the omnipotent god of, providing it sustenance through our pain and dissolution through our imperceptible modulation toward indifference. The pain allows us into the truth of our emotions in earnest, not distorted by this grey-scale shift of memory. Memory is not material, but like it, it tends toward entropy when time is the ultimate force of our existence. Time is a capitalist demon and in our masochism, and hence our Batailleian transgression, we can subvert it and manuver through our worlds without their decay from external entity. This is not to suggest change is impossible, nor non-useful, but it is utilised in the sense of moving for purely productive reasons. Dilute your reality formed by suffering as to transfer your soul into flesh, to re-utilise the excess energy spent into servile ends. In some ways, this piece is anti-change.

As I was moving boxes today I found myself very frustrated by pottery and I couldn’t convey why. I realised later it was an incompatibility with my perception of “important art” or “important change”. I want to learn pottery, but I would want to do so to produce “important art” which I realised means I never could. Things are only important in retrospect. You must enjoy creation and humiliation in learning to create, and you must have the soul of a creator. My agony and stress around pottery was seemingly just grappling with the fact that this is not me. I cannot produce anything of value with this mentality, because those who produce it can only do so by virtue of their spirit’s tendency. Pottery is impenetrable to my lust for recognition, because it demands time and failure, and intense spectacle in the process of failure, a literal monument to it. Maybe it’s because human history can be visualised through important pottery, and rarely its failures. Maybe my infinite production of failures until I die will render my life as never having occurred. When I know someone is good at pottery, or is in the process of making something, I feel a spiteful jealousy, because their passion escaped the confines of their mental solitude, and I can’t jump that hurdle by just moving. That kind of change is too rooted in a wound to my soul, to a need to create the end product rather than simply create and have the end product be the result.
<br></br>
I want to do pottery, slowly and beautifully, because I want to do pottery. Not to break the convention of pottery and produce something entirely new. But I also want to be praised like a child, which moving is a part of too, to be seen as having evolved. I do not deserve to be praised for simple pottery, but I crave it nonetheless and feel petulant when it doesn’t arrive. I’m not sure praise was missing in my childhood, because childhood doesn’t really feel like an experience that was had by the same person at the old age of 22. I was remembering recently sitting somewhere, in some city, on a school trip. We were there to be organised and marched elsewhere, maybe to an aquarium. I could see stores that I wanted to inspect, and couldn’t, bound by the fact that I was now sitting and It Was Time For Lunch. You can physically go anywhere and do anything, but you can’t will yourself to very easily at all. Really, its quite difficult to do anything. I remember feeling tired for most of these scenarios, but I guess that could just be me imprinting my current being onto the experience of that child. Childhood is really just vignettes that only emerge from the soup of memory parallel to your current emotional experience as a kind of literary device to make those emotions meaningful given they have the context of some chronological evolution and thus complexity. We’re probably more simple than that though. Anyway, back to the point, I think I’m always looking for someone to pat me on the head and tell me I made something good, it could be something as simple as someone acknowledging my existence productively, but maybe, artistically. To be some misunderstood artist that paradoxically has never really made anything is the essence of where I have moved, after all. 
The first line of this paragraph was a lie, that’s really why I want to do pottery. 
<br></br>
I’ve been working on posters lately, and no one really comments on them in any way. My childish reaction to this is to feel they are terrible and I feel my motivation to go on dwindle. There is some vision behind them, but my production is too nascent to really have an identity, and of course, being a computer scientist and someone purely motivated by the end product, there isn’t really any artistic purity behind them. They exist kind of as a demand to be seen, which is the laziest form of art, but probably why I drifted towards posters. Because their purpose is to demand to be seen. Not really conducive to success from a guy who has no design background but a lot of loudmouthed blabbering that might make you think otherwise. Everything is about maintaining that aforementioned illusion. I will never forget in primary school we needed to make covers for our creative writing, and mine was so awful the teacher asked if I wanted to redo it. I remember rushing it too, because I couldn’t bear that my perfect mental vision didn’t exist yet. 
<br></br>
Its probably not bad to be a jack of all trades master of none at the ripe age of 22, when you are full of verve and passion for existing, but you can’t do that to seem like some rare beast of creative energy, you’ve gotta actually make instead of knowing lots about video games. 
<br></br>
So I’m not sure what change is. Its not visible through a piece of art, though, that I’m sure of. Perhaps it is through some chronology of creations, but even then that implies some linearity with time which is reductive of humanity. My answer to change after moving remains unresolved, but now that pub is white in my memory, and it disgusts me.
