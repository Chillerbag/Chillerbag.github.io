As I was showering tonight I realised there wasn’t probably a waking hour last week I did not spend consuming some form of what I consider to be art. It was done in a manic daze, wanting to attain singular focus so that I could get something done, without sacrificing my wide body of knowledge of art and the goings-on of the world. All the while I was showeiring, I had a speaker blaring out Fourth World volume 1 by Jon Hassel and Brian Eno, which is a kind of genre I’ve been interested in lately, but haven’t consumed in earnest and with understanding of the roots of the genre. In the 1844 Manuscripts, in his analysis of Shakespeare, Marx recognises “the extent of the power of money is the extent of [your] power”, therefore, if one is brainless but money is the real brain of things, it follows that they possess all human capacity through their capital. The corollary of this argument is that which is unable to be performed with the human’s essential powers, is now able to be performed with money, and hence the these powers have been turned into their contrary. Money is creative power, as it wills into existence, but creative power can also be bastardised as a kind of currency, a kind of validation that assumes the ability to summon greater art. He who buys bravery may be brave though he is actually a coward. The point being, money is a distorting aspect of individuality. It’s effective power implies the negation of all attributes. This is why independent film and experimental art is so admirable, it is not these impotent chimeric simulations of a reality, demonic entities formed by some accrual of financial or intellectual currency, but the real essential abstract notions of being a human. In my excess consumption of these things, I hope to approach such verisimilitude, though my accrual; the power vested in my body of knowledge raised in discussions about film as a kind of currency merit, deems this strictly impossible.

When I saw Koyaaniqatsi last year, I was struck by the fact that the creation of art is a bastardization of life, and in capturing its beauty, we are simultaneously destroying it, unable to appreciate it in earnest because art of it has more perceived value. It is something holy, yet also digestable in the sense that it becomes some kind of energy that allows further art to exist. It infected me with the insipid idea that i needed to consume enough of the "right" art, that of enough intellectual value, to be able to spend in gloriously in the production of art of value, despite my total lack of technical prowess, in a permanent downward dilution of real existence.
<br></br>
Rather, there is a need to exist in life without converting art into the currency which supposedly makes you rich enough to produce something of value. That is, not living to consume such a body of works that the inevitable pastiche of your own production has such breadth in its construction to make its references imperceptible, and hence something new: something important, as this is impossible. The abstract notion of human life will always fail to be accurately conveyed due to the incompatibility of the extract with the sensuous; ideas gain entropy in their conversion to sight, sound, and taste. But we must not fall for the trap that there needs to be authority in this attempt. Language itself is the base art-form, by which we attempt to convey something about the lived experience. It is innately a failure as living is simply too abstract. It could be said that any movie is bourgeois, because its status as a piece of art simply by virtue of its existence attempts to exclude those by some qualifier inept at its creation, similar to Marx’s arguments in favour of freedom of the press. In exclusion, we are discounting elements of the human experience to delineate between high and low art, just as journalists often bastardise the human project as something they are the sole documenter of, and hence highlighting the need for a newspaper of the people. 
<br></br>
More anecdotally, and maybe this is some kind of anthropological prompt, my consumption of art tends to be the breadth of ocean and depth of a puddle, because that seems most conducive to social validity. If I am well read, I am someone of value, regardless of the quality of my understanding. If I have listened to lots of music, there is a greater chance there will be the harmonic glimmer in the eyes of another who has finally found someone who listens to the same band. My consumption is a kind of intellectual prostitution, which like capital, I don’t see the full return on in the name of someone else’s social profit. These conversions of the abstract; knowledge into currency, makes us specific expressions of ourselves and our relations to nature and each other impotent in that they are instead transactional valuations of merit, of exploitability. Marx invites to assume “the relationship to the world to be a human one” and in such a case, love can only be exchanged for love; art can only exist in its artistic value.

This is pretty waxy, so whats the actual learning here in regard to being alive and enjoying art? Well, enjoying should not be a project in and of itself, it doesn’t need to be this curated reflection of yourself vis a vis the diary on your letterboxd reflecting your soul. The asceticism associated with enjoyment is that of wretched nobility, that social circles are more able to “enjoy” in their supreme society and their intellectual class disposition. In these cases, like in universities, enjoyment becomes a philosophy through it only existing as an academia. Enjoyment became its economic counterpart according to Marx in The German Ideology – luxury. The actual conditions of enjoyment as a philosophy only exist outside of class and the social relations which inform them, which inevitably requires as socialist mindset. It is difficult then, to reject the feverish consumption, as it is difficult to reject the society in which you live. But, I believe art can only be produced from an account of living, which art doesn’t directly inform, and instead seeks to account for. So regardless of its unproductive labour, walking in a park is enough, even if it doesn’t convert to a review or a really smart blog post. It’s allowing myself to not spiral into indecision at the risk it poses, that of unproductivity, that is the challenge.
