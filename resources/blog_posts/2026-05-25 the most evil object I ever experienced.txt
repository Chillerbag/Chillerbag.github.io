When I was in year 9 camp, one of the activities we did was a giant swing. This giant swing was outside Fulham Prison, in central Gippsland. I remember the brief pause at the apex of the initial swing, the euphoria of having been in control of my own height and descent. Still, constrained by obvious geometry, but controlling the general situation. I could see straight into a prison yard that seemed like it was a kind of “reintegration back into society” style of prison where they simulated a generic suburb. I saw a guy in prison clothing, and maybe this is re-fabricating the actual memory, but I could have sworn he was in a single colour jumpsuit. Maybe green. There wasn’t really a profundity to it at the time. I was here, in the sky, and they could see me from over there. I think they instructed us not to wave at them, but I felt a temptation to. Half because I did want to taunt them, and half because I did want to somehow transfer something beyond language, like “we’re the same”. Not in a pseudo-intellectual sense of having said that we are both in a kind of prison, but that we are both humans despite how we have been categorised.
<br></br>
I don’t think they wanted to look at the giant swing.
<br></br>
I was reminded of it recently by a friend, and it did send me to a kind of panic. Why would they have wanted us to see that? There had to be some subliminal messaging that probably induced a kind of unambiguous fear at the boundaries of society that could be easily transgressed. That the brief joy I was experiencing, of the wind rushing through my hair and the possibility of reaching the sky again, could be utterly removed from me forever. I was right in that there were something similar to us, but it is that there is a thin veil, a leyline realm above geography and society and land, where a psychic deity of our collective anxiety reaches its tendrils to guide my motions, stabbing me to induce a waking death should I stray from its order. There isn’t a duality of freedom, no, there is a state of perpetual fear for all. I realised, looking at it on google maps, that the swing was actually in the prison. Not near it. The prison is also a private prison. How did my school get in touch with these guys? Did they offer to show them? There’s an obvious social benefit to some power here, to remind me of all they can take and to make me live by that syntax, to make me somnolent and easy to deal with in my enfeebled being, rendered useless by fear of the jail beneath my soaring childish feet, so far down that if I were to land there I would be dead. 
<br></br>
The giant swing was a monolithic totem to this god of paradoxically ordered freedom. It limits my reach to the sky, just enough that as I reach what feels like true freedom, from that cliff face which stands triumphant over a boundless and fertile steppe, I can see into the chasm which I cannot cross to reach it. A chasm from which no one can ever return, brandished as a subhuman for choosing to defile the piety to our brilliant totem of youth, of memories of camp where we first saw someone dying in the most prolonged and metaphysical manner we can legally induce. 
<br></br>
On the Prisons Victoria site, they talk about labour in prisons. They make it a point that the labour can fluctuate to accommodate whatever industry is in demand. The freedoms that we exercise revolve around maintaining a job where our labour is exploited by virtue of profit existing at all, and the natural hand of the market tells us if we are necessary and whether we should enforce the same dynamism of the prison labour system upon ourselves. We are again equalised in the similarity of our freedoms, just that a small fraction of our value becomes expendable, where a minute fraction does for prisoners. They just can’t go higher on the swing. Their hair won’t move at those kind of speeds and they won’t see the Gippsland farms stretching far enough that they seem to bend with the curvature of the earth. As life goes on, the maximum height grows lower and lower. 
<br></br>
This pillar is one that grazes the heaven of the free market, it beckons us toward heights horizontal and vertical, whilst precisely defining the limitations like a cage. Just subtle enough that we can see god and not what he makes us do.
